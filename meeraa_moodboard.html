03 — Fabrics & Textiles

The Craft Story

Fabric 01 — Rajasthan Heirloom

Bandhani

Ancient resist-dyeing technique where the fabric is tied in thousands of tiny knots before dyeing — each knot creating a precise dot of undyed fabric. The result is a dense constellation of white or contrasting dots on a deep, saturated ground. In this collection, Bandhani appears in Anarkalis, Lehengas, and Suit Sets — bold, structured silhouettes that honour the textile's drama.

Deep Red / Rani Pink Indigo / Peacock Tie-Dye Dots Anarkali · Lehenga · Suit Sets Heritage craft — Jaipur & Jodhpur
Fabric 02 — The Teej Fabric

Leheriya

Diagonal wave-stripe dyeing of Jaipur — the word itself comes from "leher" (wave). Applied on rolled cotton fabric, each diagonal stripe flows from corner to corner. Leheriya is the Teej fabric — women across Rajasthan wear it specifically during Sawan and the Teej festival season in saffron, green, and yellow. In this collection, it appears in Kurtas and A-line styles — flowing, lighter silhouettes that move beautifully outdoors.

Teej Green / Saffron / Yellow Diagonal Wave Stripe Kurta · A-line · Dupatta The official fabric of Sawan season
Embellishment — Jaipur's Golden Thread

Gota Patti

Flat gold or silver ribbon woven into intricate borders, motifs, and edging. Gota Patti is Jaipur's signature embellishment — born in the royal workshops of Amber and carried through centuries of artisan craft. In this collection it appears as border detailing on dupattas, necklines, and hemlines — catching light beautifully against the natural cotton ground. Every outdoor location in this shoot was chosen partly for how gota gold reads against its backdrop.

Gold Ribbon Work Border · Neckline · Dupatta hem All garment types Light-catching — best at golden hour

05 — Special Feature

The Teej Module

Hariyali Teej is the soul context of this entire shoot. Leheriya is worn by women across Rajasthan specifically during Sawan — making this collection not just fashion, but cultural participation. This module covers four distinct Teej shooting opportunities, each a different visual grammar.

5A — The Jhaki: Shooting Inside the Royal Procession

The Teej procession begins at City Palace and moves through Tripolia Gate → Chhoti Chaupar → Gangauri Bazaar. The procession includes the golden palanquin of Goddess Teej Mata, royal guards, folk dancers, decorated camels, and military bands. Models dressed in Leheriya outfits are photographed within the crowd lining the route — not staging the procession, but existing inside it. They look like festival participants, not models. The palanquin, marigold-decked arches, and old city's pink walls become the background.

Practical note: Keep the team small — one photographer, one assistant, three models. No tripod, no reflector. Scout the route on the day before and identify 3 anchor spots along Gangauri Bazaar (arched doorways make the best frame). Models arrive already dressed; no onset changes. Shoot window is 5–8 PM on the procession evening.

Leheriya looks only Team of 5 max Scout route day before Documentary style · no poses

One model on the swing (in Leheriya green-yellow), two models beside her laughing — this is both a Teej ritual reference and a natural group composition. 5C — The Teej Puja Setup: Group Ritual Shot

A group shot rooted in the actual Hariyali Teej puja ritual. Three models seated together around a traditional puja arrangement — a decorated brass kalash with mango leaves and coconut, a brass thali with diya, marigold and rose flowers, kumkum, and sindoor. One model performing aarti, one with hands joined in prayer, one with mehendi hands holding flower petals. This is the most emotionally resonant shot concept — it's devotion, sisterhood, and festive beauty all in one frame.

Best set outdoors against the wall of Sisodia Rani Ka Bagh (painted with Krishna murals) or in a haveli courtyard. Late afternoon light, approximately 4 PM.

12 — Post-Production Direction

The Grade & Edit Philosophy

Color Grade — Overall Direction

Warm, earthy, saturated. The Rajasthani palette should sing — deepen the Bandhani reds and Leheriya greens/saffrons. Lift the shadows just enough to preserve sandstone warmth. Keep skin tones rich and true — no over-brightening, no bleaching. Think: Rajasthani miniature painting translated to photography.

Retouching Philosophy

This is a plus-size brand that celebrates bodies as they are. Retouching philosophy: skin texture kept natural, no body reshaping whatsoever, only colour and light refinement. Meera's audience responds most to authentic, unmanipulated bodies. This is both ethical and commercial.

Per-Context Grade

  • Heritage locations — warm golden, slight film grain, lifted shadows
  • Teej procession — high saturation, vibrant festival colours, slight contrast boost
  • Desert (Kishanbagh) — sandy amber tones, deep shadows, cinematic
  • Garden (Sisodia Rani) — lush greens, soft morning light, romantic glow

Output Formats

  • E-commerce: white/neutral bg isolation, clean product view
  • Campaign: full landscape editorial, 16:9 and 4:5 crops
  • Instagram Grid: square 1:1 crop for feed cohesion
  • Reels: 9:16 vertical, shot list includes vertical-frame versions
  • Lookbook PDF: double-spread landscape masters
